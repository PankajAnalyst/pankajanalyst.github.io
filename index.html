About

The short version? I started in
telecom, wandered into HR,
and found my edge in data.

My career didn't follow a straight line — and honestly, that's the whole point. I started as a Network Engineer at Ericsson, laying cable and debugging signal issues. Good work, but it wasn't where my brain wanted to live. What kept pulling me in was the people side: who's getting hired, who's staying, what's actually making teams tick.

So I moved into HR. Spent years doing the full-cycle thing — recruitment, vendor management, performance tracking, the works. But the part I kept gravitating toward was the numbers underneath it all. Nobody was building dashboards for hiring funnels or bench utilization. Everybody was running on gut feel and spreadsheets that were three weeks stale. So I started building them myself.

Four years of Power BI later, I've shipped 6 production dashboards across BCG, CNH Industrial, and Nokia — covering everything from recruitment pipelines to commercial billing to attrition early warnings. The thing that makes my work different is simple: I've sat in the HR meetings, heard the questions leadership actually asks, and then went back and built the exact views that answer them. I don't build dashboards for analysts — I build them for the people making the calls.

01 Hiring Was Bleeding Out Before Anyone Noticed
BCG CNH Industrial SQL + DAX
+
The situation

Hiring bottlenecks were invisible until SLAs had already slipped. The recruitment team was running on email threads and weekly Excel updates — by the time someone flagged a stuck position, it had been aging for three weeks. Nobody could answer "where are we losing candidates?" without manually pulling data from two systems.

What I built

A real-time multi-stage funnel connecting SQL server and Excel sources. Tracked stage conversion, time-in-stage, drop-off rates, and aging profiles — with both client and internal interview feeds flowing into one view. Used 12+ custom DAX measures to calculate rolling averages, stage velocity, and offer-to-join probability. The whole thing refreshed daily instead of weekly.

What changed

03 Three Days Every Month, Just to Send Invoices
11+ MSAs SQL
+
The situation

The finance team was spending three days every month manually reconciling vendor MSAs and project billing — and still making errors. Rate cards lived in contract PDFs, deployed headcount was tracked in Excel, and project timelines were managed via email. Nobody had a single view of what we were owed versus what we'd invoiced.

Added DAX measures for invoice value by client, PO utilization tracking, revenue vs. target comparisons, and overdue payment flags. Built it so finance could slice by MSA, project, or time period without touching a spreadsheet.

The situation

Monthly business reviews had no data backbone. Managers were walking into MBRs with scattered spreadsheets and anecdotal evidence. How I Work

From messy data to Monday-morning dashboards.

This is the framework I follow every time — whether the ask is a recruitment funnel or a billing reconciliation.

01

Listen to the Meeting

I sit in the review where the questions come up. What's leadership actually asking? What data are they pulling up on the spot? That's where the dashboard spec lives.

02

Audit the Sources

Map every data source — SQL tables, HRIS exports, Excel trackers, ATS feeds. Figure out what's clean, what's messy, and what's just not being captured yet.

03

Model It Right

Build a proper star schema. Get the relationships, grain, and DAX measures right the first time so the dashboard doesn't break when data scales.

04

Build for the User

Design the dashboard for the person who'll open it at 8:30am — not for the analyst. Clear KPIs up top, drill-throughs for detail, nothing that requires a training session.

05

Ship & Iterate

Deploy to Power BI Service, set up scheduled refresh, train the team. Then watch how they actually use it — the second version is always better than the first.
